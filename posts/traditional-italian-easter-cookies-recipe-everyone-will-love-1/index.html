Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise That Melt in Your Mouth

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 7, 2026
20 min Prep Time
15 min Time
24 Servings
Medium Difficulty

These soft Italian Easter cookies are lightly sweet with a subtle anise flavor that makes them unforgettable. They’re perfect for holiday gatherings or a cozy morning coffee treat—always a family favorite!

Pro Tips

Always sift your dry ingredients together before mixing.
Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes baking powder and salt, which helps the cookies rise uniformly and prevents clumps. Skipping this step can cause uneven texture and dense spots.
Cream butter and sugar until light and fluffy.
This process incorporates air that expands during baking, creating a tender cookie. If you don’t cream well, the cookies may turn out flat and heavy.
Add eggs one at a time and mix thoroughly.
Gradually incorporating eggs prevents the batter from curdling and ensures a smooth, even mixture. Rushing this can cause separation and uneven texture.
Alternate adding dry ingredients and milk.
This technique keeps the dough moist and prevents overmixing, which can develop gluten and make cookies tough.
Use a cookie scoop for uniform size and even baking.
Consistent cookie sizes ensure they bake evenly and finish at the same time, avoiding some being over or undercooked.
Watch baking time carefully to keep centers soft.
Overbaking dries out the cookies. Pulling them out when edges are golden but centers still soft results in that melt-in-your-mouth texture.
Cool cookies on the baking sheet before moving.
This allows cookies to set and prevents breakage when transferring to a rack.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the dough

Why it happens Many home bakers think more mixing means better blending, but overmixing develops gluten, making cookies tough instead of tender.

Fix Mix just until ingredients are combined. Use gentle folding motions especially when adding flour. Stop mixing as soon as the dough looks uniform.

Skipping sifting of dry ingredients

Why it happens It’s an extra step that sometimes feels unnecessary, but not sifting can lead to clumps and uneven leavening.

Fix Always sift flour, baking powder, and salt together to aerate and evenly distribute ingredients for the best texture.

Not creaming butter and sugar enough

Why it happens Rushing or using cold butter can cause this step to fail, resulting in dense cookies.

Fix Make sure butter is fully softened. Cream with sugar for at least 3-5 minutes until light and fluffy.

Using old baking powder

Why it happens Expired baking powder loses potency, so cookies don’t rise well.

Fix Check baking powder freshness by mixing a small amount in hot water; it should bubble vigorously. Replace if not.

Overbaking the cookies

Why it happens Leaving cookies in too long dries them out and ruins the soft texture.

Fix Remove cookies when edges are golden but centers still look soft. They firm up while cooling.

Variations

Lemon-Anise Italian Easter Cookies

Add 1 tablespoon of freshly grated lemon zest to the dough for a bright citrus twist that pairs beautifully with the anise flavor. This variation adds a fresh aroma and a slight tang that livens up the cookie.

Note: Add lemon zest with the wet ingredients to evenly distribute the flavor without affecting dough texture.

Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies

Swap all-purpose flour for a gluten-free blend suitable for baking. The texture will be a bit more crumbly but still tender and delicious, perfect for those with gluten sensitivities.

Note: Use a blend that contains xanthan gum or add 1/2 teaspoon separately to mimic gluten’s binding properties.

Anise Seed Crunch Cookies

Instead of anise extract, add 1 teaspoon of toasted, crushed anise seeds for a stronger, slightly crunchy texture and more pronounced flavor. This gives a rustic feel to the classic cookie.

Note: Toast seeds lightly in a dry pan before crushing to enhance their aroma.

Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies

Once cooled, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. This adds richness and a bit of indulgence to the traditional cookies, making them perfect for gifts or special occasions.

Note: Use good-quality chocolate and chill dipped cookies on parchment paper until firm.

Honey-Sweetened Italian Easter Cookies

Replace half the granulated sugar with honey for a more natural sweetness and moist texture. Honey adds floral notes that complement the anise beautifully.

Note: Reduce milk slightly to compensate for added liquid from honey.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store these cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain their tenderness and prevent drying out.

Refrigerator

You can refrigerate the cookies in an airtight container for up to 7 days. Refrigeration may slightly firm the texture, so bring them to room temperature before serving for the best flavor and softness.

Freezer

Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe bag or container for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before eating.

Reheating

To refresh cookies, warm them in a 300°F oven for 5 minutes or microwave for 10-15 seconds. Avoid overheating as it can dry them out. A light mist of water before warming helps retain moisture.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the dough up to 24 hours ahead and keep it refrigerated, wrapped tightly. Shape and bake the cookies the day you plan to serve them for freshest results.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with another flavor?
Yes, you can use vanilla extract as a milder alternative, but it will change the traditional anise flavor. Some people also like substituting with almond extract, which pairs well but results in a different taste profile. The anise is key to the classic flavor, so try to keep that if you want the authentic experience.
Why are my cookies turning out dry and crumbly?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or using too much flour. Make sure to measure flour correctly (spoon and level method) and watch your baking time closely. Also, avoid overmixing the dough, which can develop gluten and make cookies tough.
Can I use anise seeds instead of extract?
Yes, but toast and grind the seeds finely before adding to the dough for best flavor. Seeds provide a stronger, slightly crunchy texture. Using seeds will make the flavor more pronounced but can add a bit of grit if not ground well.
How do I store these cookies to keep them fresh?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature away from heat and light. They keep well for about 5 days. For longer storage, freeze them properly wrapped and thaw before serving to maintain softness.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
You can try replacing butter with vegan margarine and eggs with flax eggs or applesauce, but texture and flavor may differ. The cookies might be less tender and lack some richness, so expect some variation.
What if my dough is too sticky to handle?
If dough is sticky, chill it in the fridge for 20-30 minutes. This firms up the butter and makes scooping easier. Avoid adding extra flour as it can toughen the cookies.
Why do my cookies spread too much?
Cookies spread too much if the butter is too warm or you use too much sugar. Make sure butter is softened but not melted. Also, chilling the dough before baking helps control spreading.
Can I make the dough ahead of time?
Yes, the dough can be refrigerated for up to 24 hours before baking. This resting time actually improves flavor and texture by allowing the ingredients to meld.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3 large eggs
  • 2 teaspoons anise extract
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup whole milk
  • Powdered sugar, for dusting

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C) and lining two baking sheets with parchment paper. This ensures even baking and prevents sticking. Sifting the flour, baking powder, and salt together is important because it aerates the flour and helps distribute the leavening agent evenly, which contributes to a lighter cookie texture. Skipping this step might result in denser cookies or uneven rising, so take a moment to do it right.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flour mixture should be light and lump-free after sifting
Step 2
In a large bowl, cream the softened butter with granulated sugar until the mixture is pale and fluffy—this usually takes about 3-5 minutes with a hand mixer. Creaming incorporates air into the butter, which helps the cookies rise and become tender. If you rush this step or don’t cream enough, the cookies can turn out flat and tough.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugar should look light in color and fluffy in texture
Step 3
Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. Then stir in the anise extract. Adding eggs one by one ensures they emulsify properly with the butter and sugar, creating a smooth batter. This prevents curdling and keeps the dough uniform. The anise extract adds that signature flavor; don’t skip it!
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter should be smooth and glossy with no streaks of egg
Step 4
Gradually add the sifted dry ingredients to the wet mixture, alternating with the milk. Start and end with the dry ingredients. Mixing in portions helps maintain the batter’s structure and prevents overmixing, which can make cookies tough. Use a spatula to fold gently until just combined—overworking the dough is a common mistake here.
⏱ Time: 5-7 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough should be smooth but slightly thick, not runny
Step 5
Once the dough is ready, use a tablespoon or cookie scoop to drop rounded balls onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. This spacing lets the cookies spread without merging. If you crowd them, they'll bake into one giant cookie, which isn’t what you want!
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Evenly spaced dough balls on baking sheet
Step 6
Bake the cookies in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes. Keep an eye on them starting at 12 minutes; you want the edges to be just golden while the centers remain soft. Overbaking dries them out, so remove them promptly. The cookies will firm up a bit as they cool.
⏱ Time: 12-15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are lightly golden, centers look set but soft
Step 7
Let the cookies cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring them to a wire rack. This resting time helps the cookies set up so they don’t break apart. Moving them too soon can cause them to crumble.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies feel firm enough to handle without breaking
Step 8
Once completely cool, dust the cookies lightly with powdered sugar for a pretty finish and a touch of extra sweetness. This step is optional but adds a lovely classic look. Use a fine sieve to get an even dusting.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Even, light coating of powdered sugar over cookies
Step 9
Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. They stay tender and flavorful, and the anise scent actually mellows beautifully over time. If you want to keep them longer, freezing is an option—just thaw at room temperature before serving.
⏱ Time: N/A
✓ Doneness: Cookies remain soft and aromatic when stored properly

Final Thoughts

Give these Italian Easter cookies a try—you’ll love how tender and flavorful they are. Don’t forget to share your baking stories and photos with friends and family!
