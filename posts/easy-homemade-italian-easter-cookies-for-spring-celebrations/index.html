Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Subtle Anise Twist

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 7, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
24 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These soft Italian Easter cookies are a springtime favorite, lightly sweet with a lovely hint of anise. They bake up golden and tender, perfect for family brunches or sharing with friends on holiday mornings.

Pro Tips

Don’t overmix the dough once you add the flour.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which can make the cookies tough and dense instead of soft and tender. Mix just until the flour disappears to keep the texture light and delicate.
Use room temperature ingredients for best results.
Butter and eggs at room temperature blend together more easily, creating a smoother dough and better emulsification. Cold ingredients can cause the dough to curdle or mix unevenly, leading to inconsistent texture.
Line your baking sheets with parchment paper or silicone mats.
This prevents sticking and promotes even baking. It also makes cleanup easier and helps the cookies keep their shape without spreading too thin or burning on the bottom.
Let the cookies cool on the baking sheet a few minutes before transferring.
This resting time allows the cookies to firm up slightly, reducing the risk of breaking when moving them to a cooling rack. It also helps maintain their soft interior.
Use a cookie scoop for uniform cookie sizes.
Even-sized cookies bake more evenly, ensuring consistent texture and color. Different sizes bake at different rates, which can result in some cookies being over or underbaked.
Don’t skip sifting the flour and baking powder.
Sifting aerates these ingredients and removes lumps, which helps create a lighter dough and prevents uneven distribution of leavening, avoiding odd textures or tastes.
Add a light dusting of powdered sugar after cooling for a pretty finish.
Powdered sugar adds a subtle sweetness and visual appeal without overpowering the anise flavor. It also gives the cookies a delicate, festive look perfect for holiday sharing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overbaking the cookies

Why it happens Many home cooks leave the cookies in the oven too long, thinking they need to be fully browned all over. This often happens because they’re unfamiliar with the soft texture these cookies should have.

Fix Watch closely starting at 12 minutes and remove the cookies when the edges are lightly golden but the centers still look slightly soft. The cookies will firm up as they cool, so it’s better to underbake slightly than overbake.

Using cold butter or eggs

Why it happens Sometimes ingredients are used straight from the fridge, which can prevent proper mixing and emulsification, leading to a curdled or uneven dough.

Fix Plan ahead and bring your butter and eggs to room temperature before starting. This ensures they blend smoothly, creating a uniform and tender dough.

Skipping sifting of dry ingredients

Why it happens Skipping sifting is common when people want to rush, but it can lead to lumps or uneven leavening.

Fix Always sift the flour and baking powder together to aerate the mixture and ensure even distribution. This helps the cookies rise and bake evenly.

Overmixing after adding flour

Why it happens Some cooks think mixing longer will create a better dough, but this activates gluten, making cookies tough.

Fix Mix only until the flour is just incorporated. Stop as soon as you see no more dry streaks to keep the cookies soft.

Crowding the baking sheet

Why it happens Placing dough balls too close causes them to bake into each other, resulting in misshapen cookies.

Fix Space the dough balls at least 2 inches apart to allow for spreading and even heat circulation.

Variations

Lemon Zest Italian Easter Cookies

Add 1 tablespoon of freshly grated lemon zest to the dough for a bright, citrusy twist. This variation brings a fresh flavor that complements the anise beautifully, making the cookies even more refreshing for springtime.

Note: Add the zest along with the anise extract to the wet ingredients. No change in baking time needed, but watch for a slightly brighter aroma.

Almond-Flavored Italian Easter Cookies

Substitute half the flour with finely ground almond meal and add 1/2 teaspoon almond extract. This adds a lovely nutty richness and a slightly denser texture that’s still soft and tender.

Note: Almond meal absorbs more moisture, so you may need to add a tablespoon more milk if dough feels dry.

Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies

Use a 1:1 gluten-free baking flour blend in place of all-purpose flour. The cookies will keep their softness but may be a bit more delicate. Perfect for those avoiding gluten yet wanting to enjoy this classic treat.

Note: Check that your baking powder is gluten-free. Bake as usual but handle dough gently to avoid crumbling.

Anise Seed Italian Easter Cookies

Instead of extract, fold in 1 teaspoon of toasted anise seeds for a more textured anise flavor. The seeds add a subtle crunch and aromatic boost that’s traditional in some Italian regions.

Note: Toast seeds lightly in a dry pan before adding. This brings out their oils and enhances flavor.

Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies

Once cooled, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. This adds a rich contrast to the soft, anise-flavored cookie and makes them extra special for gifting.

Note: Use good quality chocolate and chill dipped cookies until firm. Store in a cool place to prevent melting.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight and heat to maintain their soft texture. Avoid stacking too many layers without parchment paper between to prevent sticking.

Refrigerator

You can refrigerate the cookies in an airtight container for up to 7 days. The cooler temperature will firm them up, so bring to room temperature before serving for the best softness and flavor.

Freezer

Freeze the cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving to restore texture.

Reheating

If you want to refresh the cookies after refrigeration or freezing, warm them in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes. This softens them without drying out. Avoid microwaving, which can make them rubbery or too soft.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the dough 1 day ahead and refrigerate it tightly wrapped. Shape and bake the cookies the next day for the freshest texture. Alternatively, bake in advance and freeze baked cookies for convenient holiday treats.

FAQ

Can I use anise seeds instead of anise extract?
Yes! Toasting about 1 teaspoon of anise seeds lightly in a pan and then grinding them before adding to the dough works well. It gives a slightly different texture and a more robust anise flavor. Just be sure to toast seeds gently to release oils without burning.
Why are my cookies turning out hard instead of soft?
Hard cookies usually result from overbaking or overmixing the dough. Watch the baking time carefully and remove the cookies when edges are just golden. Also, mix the dough only until ingredients are combined to avoid gluten development that causes toughness.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
To make a vegan version, substitute butter with a vegan butter alternative and use flax eggs (1 tablespoon ground flaxseed + 3 tablespoons water per egg). The texture will be slightly different but still delicious. Use plant-based milk instead of cow’s milk.
How do I store these cookies to keep them fresh?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. For longer storage, freeze the baked cookies in a sealed container for up to 3 months. Let them thaw at room temperature before eating.
Can I add other flavors besides anise?
Absolutely! Lemon zest, almond extract, or even a touch of vanilla can complement or substitute the anise flavor. Just adjust the amount so it doesn’t overpower the delicate cookie base.
Why is sifting the flour important?
Sifting aerates the flour and removes lumps, which helps create a lighter, more tender cookie. It also ensures the baking powder and salt are evenly distributed, preventing uneven rising or salty pockets.
Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
You can, but reduce or omit added salt in the recipe to avoid overly salty cookies. Unsalted butter gives you better control over the seasoning and flavor balance.
What if my dough feels too dry or crumbly?
If the dough is dry, add milk a teaspoon at a time until it holds together softly but isn’t sticky. This usually happens if flour was measured too heavily or if the butter wasn’t soft enough.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 cup milk
  • Powdered sugar for dusting (optional)

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C). In a large bowl, cream together the softened butter and granulated sugar until the mixture is light and fluffy. This step incorporates air into the dough, which helps create a tender texture in the finished cookies. Be patient and use a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed for about 3-5 minutes. Avoid rushing or skipping this step, as under-creamed butter can lead to dense cookies.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: The mixture should look pale, smooth, and fluffy.
Step 2
Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. Then add the anise extract. The eggs provide structure and moisture, while the anise brings that signature flavor. Mixing thoroughly ensures the dough is uniform, but don’t overmix here to avoid tough cookies. If you skip adding the eggs slowly, the dough might curdle or separate.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: The batter will be smooth and slightly glossy.
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. Sifting removes lumps and aerates the flour, which helps keep the cookies light. This dry mix is essential to combine evenly with the wet ingredients to avoid pockets of baking powder or salt that can cause uneven rising or flavor.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: The dry ingredients should be well blended and lump-free.
Step 4
Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, alternating with the milk. Start and end with the flour mixture. This alternating method helps prevent the dough from becoming too stiff and makes mixing easier. Mix just until combined with each addition to avoid developing gluten, which would make the cookies chewy instead of soft.
⏱ Time: 4 minutes
✓ Doneness: The dough should be soft, slightly sticky, and uniform in color.
Step 5
Line baking sheets with parchment paper or silicone mats. Using a tablespoon or small cookie scoop, drop dough balls about 2 inches apart on the sheets. Leaving space allows the cookies to spread evenly without merging. If you crowd the pan, the cookies might bake unevenly or stick together.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough balls should be uniform in size and spaced well.
Step 6
Bake the cookies in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes. Keep an eye on them starting at 12 minutes; the edges should be lightly golden while the centers remain soft. Overbaking will dry them out, so it’s better to err on the side of slightly underbaked for that tender texture.
⏱ Time: 12-15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are golden and centers look set but soft.
Step 7
Remove the cookies from the oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time lets the cookies finish baking gently and firm up enough to handle without breaking.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies hold their shape and feel slightly firm on top.
Step 8
Once completely cool, dust the cookies with powdered sugar if desired. This adds a lovely touch of sweetness and a pretty finish without overpowering the delicate anise flavor. It’s optional but highly recommended for presentation.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies have a light, even dusting of powdered sugar.
Step 9
Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. They stay soft and flavorful, making them a perfect make-ahead treat for holidays or gatherings. If you want to keep them longer, freeze as described in the storage section.
⏱ Time: N/A
✓ Doneness: Cookies remain soft and fresh when stored properly.
