Cozy Homemade Chicken Soup with Fresh Veggies and Tender Meat

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 23, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This homemade chicken soup is like a warm hug on chilly days. Slow-simmered broth with fresh veggies and tender chicken makes it perfect for family dinners or when you’re feeling under the weather.

Pro Tips

Always start with cold water when making broth.
Starting with cold water allows the proteins and collagen in the bones and meat to slowly dissolve into the broth. This slow release results in a richer, more gelatinous broth. If you start with hot water, the proteins coagulate and trap impurities, causing a cloudy and less flavorful broth.
Skim the foam from the broth during the first few minutes of simmering.
The foam contains impurities and coagulated proteins that can make the broth taste bitter or look cloudy. Removing it early ensures a cleaner, clearer broth and better overall flavor.
Simmer gently rather than boiling hard.
A gentle simmer extracts flavor without toughening the meat or emulsifying fats into the broth. Boiling hard can cause a greasy, cloudy soup with chewy chicken. Slow simmering keeps the broth clear and chicken tender.
Add fresh herbs at the end of cooking.
Fresh herbs like parsley lose their bright flavor and color if cooked too long. Adding them just before serving preserves their freshness and adds a vibrant note to the soup.
Cool the soup before refrigerating or freezing.
Cooling prevents condensation and bacterial growth in storage containers. Hot soup can raise the temperature inside your fridge or freezer, risking food safety and altering texture upon reheating.
Use a fine mesh sieve to strain the broth.
Straining removes vegetable bits and impurities, resulting in a smooth, clear broth that looks and tastes more refined. Skipping this step can leave unwanted texture and cloudiness.
Shred chicken after it cools slightly.
Shredding hot chicken is difficult and can lead to uneven pieces or burns. Waiting until it’s cool enough to handle makes shredding easier and safer, resulting in uniform, tender pieces.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Boiling the soup rapidly instead of simmering gently.

Why it happens Many cooks rush the process, thinking higher heat speeds up cooking, but this causes tough meat and cloudy broth.

Fix Keep the heat low to maintain a gentle simmer. Adjust the burner so you see small bubbles occasionally, not a rolling boil. Use a lid partially covering the pot to control evaporation.

Skipping the skimming step during initial cooking.

Why it happens It’s easy to overlook skimming foam, especially when multitasking in the kitchen.

Fix Set a timer to remind yourself to check the pot during the first 10 minutes. Use a slotted spoon to gently remove foam as it forms. This keeps broth clean and flavorful.

Not straining the broth before serving.

Why it happens Some think leaving veggies in adds heartiness, but it can make the soup gritty or bitter after long cooking.

Fix Strain the broth through a fine sieve before adding shredded chicken back in. This produces a smooth texture and balanced flavor.

Adding fresh herbs too early.

Why it happens New cooks often add herbs with the vegetables, not realizing they lose flavor and color with long cooking.

Fix Add parsley and other fresh herbs only in the last few minutes or just before serving for maximum freshness.

Using pre-cooked noodles or rice without timing adjustments.

Why it happens Adding pre-cooked starches too early can make them mushy and unappetizing.

Fix Add noodles or rice near the end of cooking and cook just until heated through. For noodles, follow package timing carefully.

Variations

Spicy Chicken Soup

Add a diced jalapeño or a teaspoon of chili flakes during simmering for a gentle kick. Finish with a squeeze of lime and chopped cilantro for a fresh, spicy twist that warms you up from the inside out.

Note: Spicy ingredients should be added early enough to infuse but not so long that they overpower. Adjust heat level to taste.

Creamy Chicken Soup

Stir in 1/2 cup heavy cream or coconut milk after cooking to create a rich, velvety texture. This variation feels indulgent and perfect for colder days when you want something extra comforting.

Note: Add cream off heat or on very low to prevent curdling. Stir gently until fully incorporated.

Vegetable-Loaded Chicken Soup

Include diced potatoes, green beans, and peas along with the other veggies to boost nutrition and texture variety. This makes the soup heartier and colorful, great for feeding a crowd.

Note: Add firmer vegetables like potatoes early; delicate ones like peas last 5 minutes of cooking.

Herb-Infused Chicken Soup

Use fresh thyme, rosemary, or sage in addition to parsley. Tie herbs in a bundle for easy removal. These herbs add earthy, woodsy notes that deepen the flavor complexity.

Note: Remove herb bundle before serving to avoid bitter flavors from overcooking.

Gluten-Free Chicken Soup

Skip noodles or use gluten-free pasta or rice. This version keeps all the comforting flavors without gluten, perfect for sensitive eaters or those with allergies.

Note: Choose gluten-free starches that cook quickly to avoid mushiness.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Chicken soup can safely sit out covered for up to 2 hours. Beyond that, bacteria can grow rapidly, so it’s best to refrigerate promptly after serving.

Refrigerator

Store soup in airtight containers for up to 4 days. The flavor often improves after a day. Reheat gently on the stove or microwave until steaming hot. Avoid reheating multiple times to maintain quality and safety.

Freezer

Freeze in airtight, freezer-safe containers for up to 3 months. Leave some headspace for expansion. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently to restore texture. Avoid freezing noodles in the soup as they can get mushy.

Reheating

Reheat soup on the stovetop over medium-low heat, stirring occasionally. Add a splash of water or broth if it’s too thick. Avoid boiling rapidly to keep chicken tender. Microwave reheating is fine but stir halfway through for even heating.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the broth and shred chicken 1-2 days ahead, storing them separately in the fridge. Add fresh veggies and herbs right before reheating to keep them bright and crisp.

FAQ

Can I use chicken breasts instead of a whole chicken?
Yes, but the broth won’t be as rich because breasts lack bones and connective tissue. If you use breasts, consider adding a few chicken wings or thighs with bones for better flavor. Also, cook breasts gently to avoid drying out.
How long can I store leftover chicken soup?
Leftover soup keeps well in the refrigerator for up to 4 days. Make sure it’s cooled and stored in airtight containers. For longer storage, freeze it for up to 3 months. Always reheat until steaming hot before eating.
Can I make this soup in a slow cooker?
Absolutely! Place all ingredients except fresh herbs in the slow cooker. Cook on low for 6-8 hours or high for 3-4 hours. Add fresh parsley at the end. Slow cooking enhances flavors but be mindful of overcooking vegetables.
Why is my broth cloudy?
Cloudy broth usually results from boiling too hard or not skimming foam. To fix this, simmer gently and skim impurities during the first 10-15 minutes. Also, avoid stirring vigorously which can emulsify fats into the broth.
Can I freeze chicken soup with noodles in it?
It’s best to freeze soup without noodles, as they tend to become mushy when thawed. Freeze the broth and chicken separately and add fresh noodles when reheating for the best texture.
How do I make the soup spicier?
Add diced jalapeños, chili flakes, or a splash of hot sauce during cooking. Start with small amounts and adjust to taste. Fresh herbs like cilantro and a squeeze of lime at the end also enhance spicy flavors.
Can I use dried herbs instead of fresh parsley?
Dried herbs can be used but the flavor won’t be as bright or fresh. Add dried parsley during cooking since it needs time to release flavor, whereas fresh parsley should be added at the end.
What’s the best way to shred cooked chicken?
Use two forks to pull the meat apart gently once it’s cool enough to handle. Alternatively, you can use your hands or even a stand mixer on low speed for quick shredding. Avoid shredding when hot to prevent burns.

What You'll Need

Approximately 8 cups, 4 servings

  • 1 whole chicken (about 3-4 lbs), cleaned and quartered
  • 2 large carrots, peeled and sliced
  • 2 celery stalks, chopped
  • 1 large onion, quartered
  • 3 garlic cloves, smashed
  • 8 cups cold water
  • 1 teaspoon salt, adjust to taste
  • 1/2 teaspoon black pepper
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 cup chopped fresh parsley
  • Optional: 1 cup egg noodles or rice

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by placing the whole cleaned chicken quarters into a large pot. Add the cold water to cover the chicken completely. This cold start allows the proteins and collagen to slowly release into the broth, creating a richer texture. Bring the pot to a gentle simmer over medium heat — avoid boiling rapidly as it can toughen the meat and make the broth cloudy. Skim off any foam or impurities that rise to the surface in the first 10 minutes to keep your broth clear and clean-tasting.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth should be gently simmering with little foam; surface looks mostly clear after skimming
Step 2
Once the broth is simmering and skimmed, add the quartered onion, sliced carrots, chopped celery, smashed garlic, bay leaves, salt, and pepper. Lower the heat to maintain a gentle simmer and cover the pot partially to let some steam escape. This slow simmer extracts all the flavors from the vegetables and chicken bones, making the broth deeply flavorful. Rushing this step by boiling hard or covering fully can make the broth cloudy or dilute the flavors.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Vegetables are tender and chicken meat is easily falling off the bone; broth smells fragrant and rich
Step 3
Carefully remove the chicken pieces from the pot and set them aside to cool slightly. This allows you to shred the meat without burning your fingers. Strain the broth through a fine mesh sieve into another pot or large bowl to remove the vegetable solids and bay leaves. This step ensures your soup has a smooth, clear broth base. Don’t skip straining — leftover bits can muddy the texture and flavor.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth is clear and free of vegetable chunks; chicken is cool enough to handle
Step 4
Once the chicken is cool enough, shred the meat off the bones using two forks. Discard the bones and any skin if you prefer a lighter soup. Return the shredded chicken to the strained broth. This shredded chicken adds tender texture and heartiness to the soup. Avoid shredding too early while chicken is hot as it can be difficult and messy.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chicken is shredded into bite-sized pieces without any bones
Step 5
If you want to add noodles or rice, bring the broth with chicken back to a gentle simmer. Add your choice of egg noodles or precooked rice, cooking according to package instructions or until tender. This adds body and makes the soup a complete meal. Be careful not to overcook noodles as they can become mushy quickly.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Noodles are tender but still slightly firm; rice is heated through
Step 6
Stir in the chopped fresh parsley just before serving. The fresh herbs lift the soup’s flavor and add a lovely green color contrast. Adding herbs too early can cause them to lose their bright flavor and vibrant color.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Parsley looks fresh and vibrant in the broth
Step 7
Taste the soup and adjust seasoning with extra salt or pepper if needed. Because the broth reduces slightly during cooking, seasoning can vary, so always taste before serving. This final adjustment ensures the soup is perfectly balanced and delicious.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth tastes well-seasoned and balanced
Step 8
Serve the soup hot in large bowls with some crusty bread on the side. The warmth and aroma make it a comforting meal that’s perfect for cold days or when you need a little pick-me-up. Don’t forget to save leftovers — the flavors deepen overnight!
⏱ Time: 0 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is steaming and inviting
Step 9
For storing, let the soup cool completely before transferring to airtight containers. Refrigerate or freeze depending on when you plan to eat it. Proper cooling prevents bacteria growth and keeps the soup tasting fresh longer.
⏱ Time: 0 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is cooled to room temperature before storage
