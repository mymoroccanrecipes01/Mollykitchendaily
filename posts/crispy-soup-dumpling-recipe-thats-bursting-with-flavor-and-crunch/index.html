Crispy Soup Dumplings with Juicy Broth and Golden Crunch

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 30, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Medium Difficulty

These crispy soup dumplings bring a delightful crunch outside while keeping a savory broth inside. Pan-fried after steaming for that perfect golden crust. Great for dinner or a fun appetizer!

Pro Tips

Rest the dough for at least 30 minutes before rolling.
Resting allows the gluten to relax, which prevents the dough from shrinking back when rolling out. This makes it easier to create thin, even wrappers and improves the texture of the finished dumplings, making them tender rather than tough.
Chill the broth before mixing it into the filling.
Chilled broth gels slightly, which helps it stay encapsulated inside the dumpling during wrapping and cooking. If the broth is too liquid when mixing, it will leak out and cause soggy wrappers or bursting during steaming.
Seal dumplings tightly with pleats to avoid leaks.
Proper sealing traps the broth inside and keeps the filling intact. If the seal is loose or has gaps, the broth will escape during cooking, resulting in dry dumplings and a mess in the steamer.
Use a non-stick skillet for pan-frying after steaming.
A non-stick surface prevents the delicate steamed dumplings from sticking or tearing when you add oil for frying. This helps achieve a consistent golden crust without losing any dumplings to sticking or breaking.
Add water and cover the pan briefly after frying.
This step gently steams the tops again, keeping them soft and moist while preserving the crispy bottom. Skipping it results in overly dry or tough tops that don’t match the crunchy base.
Serve dumplings immediately while hot.
Crispy soup dumplings are best enjoyed fresh because the crust softens over time and the broth cools. Eating them hot ensures the textural contrast and juicy filling are at their peak.
Avoid microwaving leftover dumplings.
Microwaving often makes the dough tough and causes the broth to leak out, ruining the texture. Instead, reheat by pan-frying or steaming briefly to preserve the dumpling’s structure and juiciness.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Wrappers too thick or uneven

Why it happens Many cooks are afraid to roll the dough too thin, fearing tearing or difficulty handling, so they leave the wrappers thick.

Fix Roll the dough evenly and as thin as possible without tearing. Use light flouring and gentle rolling motions. Thin wrappers cook faster and create a better contrast with the filling.

Filling too watery causing leaks

Why it happens Adding broth that isn’t chilled or using too much liquid makes the filling runny, which leaks during cooking.

Fix Chill the broth before mixing and add gradually until the filling is sticky but not liquid. This keeps the broth inside without soaking the dough.

Under or over-steaming dumplings

Why it happens Rushing steaming leads to raw dough or filling; over-steaming makes wrappers soggy and weak.

Fix Steam for about 8 minutes over boiling water, adjusting slightly based on dumpling size. Watch for translucent wrappers and fully cooked filling.

Not sealing dumplings properly

Why it happens In a hurry or unfamiliar with pleating, cooks may leave gaps or loose seals.

Fix Take time to pleat and pinch the dough tightly. Wet edges slightly to help seal better and prevent leaks.

Skipping the pan-fry step

Why it happens Some assume steaming alone is enough or fear frying will burn the dumplings.

Fix Don’t skip pan-frying; it creates the signature crisp bottom. Use moderate heat and watch closely to avoid burning.

Variations

Shrimp and Pork Soup Dumplings

Add finely chopped shrimp to the pork filling for a sweet, briny twist. The shrimp adds a delicate seafood flavor and a slightly firmer texture, making the dumplings feel more luxurious. Keep the broth ratio the same for the juicy interior.

Note: Shrimp cooks quickly, so finely chop and mix well with pork to ensure even cooking and binding.

Vegetarian Mushroom Soup Dumplings

Replace pork with finely chopped shiitake mushrooms and tofu for a vegetarian version. Add a splash of soy sauce and mushroom broth for depth. The mushrooms provide umami richness while keeping the filling moist and flavorful.

Note: Use a thickening agent like ground nuts or soaked breadcrumbs to help trap broth inside the lighter filling.

Spicy Kimchi Soup Dumplings

Mix chopped kimchi into the pork filling for a spicy, tangy kick. This variation adds complexity and a bit of heat, perfect for those who love bold flavors. Adjust the broth seasoning to balance the acidity.

Note: Drain excess kimchi juice to prevent soggy wrappers and leaking broth.

Chicken and Ginger Soup Dumplings

Use ground chicken instead of pork and increase the grated ginger for a lighter, aromatic filling. This version is great if you want something less fatty but still juicy and flavorful.

Note: Chicken cooks faster; reduce steaming time slightly to avoid drying out the filling.

Black Garlic Soup Dumplings

Incorporate mashed black garlic into the pork filling for a sweet, mellow garlic flavor that adds richness and depth. This variation elevates the dumplings with an unexpected umami twist.

Note: Balance the sweet garlic with a splash of soy sauce and keep broth chilled for proper texture.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Dumplings can be kept at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely with a clean kitchen towel to prevent drying. Avoid leaving them out longer to reduce risk of bacterial growth.

Refrigerator

Store leftover dumplings in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 2 days. They may lose some crispness but can be reheated by pan-frying or steaming to restore texture. Avoid microwaving as it can toughen the dough.

Freezer

Freeze dumplings in a single layer on a baking sheet until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag for up to 2 months. To thaw, place in the refrigerator overnight and reheat by steaming or pan-frying gently to preserve texture and broth.

Reheating

Reheat dumplings in a non-stick skillet over medium heat with a splash of water, covering to steam and crisp the bottoms again. Avoid microwaving to prevent sogginess and broth leakage. Steaming alone also works but won’t restore crispness.

Make Ahead

You can prepare and seal dumplings up to 1 day ahead, storing them covered in the fridge before steaming and frying on the day of serving. Alternatively, freeze after sealing and cook directly from frozen, adding a few extra minutes to steaming time.

FAQ

Can I use pre-made dumpling wrappers instead of making dough from scratch?
Yes, you can use store-bought dumpling wrappers for convenience. Just ensure they are fresh and not too thick. Keep them covered while working to prevent drying out. The texture might differ slightly, but the pan-fry after steaming method still works great.
How do I prevent the broth from leaking out during cooking?
The key is chilling the broth so it gels before mixing into the filling, and sealing the dumplings tightly with pleats. Avoid overfilling and handle the wrappers gently to prevent tears. Proper steaming time also ensures the filling sets without bursting.
What’s the best way to store leftover dumplings?
Leftover dumplings store well in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 2 days. To keep the texture, reheat by pan-frying or steaming rather than microwaving. For longer storage, freeze them individually and thaw before reheating.
Can I make these dumplings vegetarian?
Absolutely! Substitute the pork with finely chopped mushrooms and tofu or other plant-based proteins. Use mushroom broth instead of chicken broth to keep the broth flavorful. Adjust seasoning to taste for a satisfying vegetarian version.
Why do some dumplings turn soggy after steaming?
Sogginess usually comes from over-steaming, too thick wrappers, or excess liquid in the filling. Steam just until the dough is translucent and the filling is cooked through. Also, avoid adding too much broth into the filling to prevent sogginess.
How thin should I roll the dough for the wrappers?
Aim for about 2-3 millimeters thickness. Thin wrappers cook faster and allow you to enjoy the broth inside without a doughy bite. However, don’t roll so thin that the dough tears or leaks during wrapping.
What oil is best for pan-frying the dumplings?
Use a neutral oil with a high smoke point like canola, vegetable, or grapeseed oil. These oils crisp up the bottoms nicely without burning or adding unwanted flavors. Avoid olive oil since it has a lower smoke point and stronger taste.
Can I freeze the dumplings before cooking?
Yes, freeze dumplings in a single layer on a tray until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag. Cook from frozen by adding a few extra minutes to steaming time. This method helps keep the broth intact and dough texture good.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 dumplings, 4 servings

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, room temperature
  • 3/4 cup boiling water
  • 1/4 cup cold water
  • 200g ground pork
  • 1/4 cup chicken broth, chilled
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon grated ginger
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon white pepper
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 tablespoon rice vinegar
  • 2 green onions, finely chopped

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by making the dough. Pour the boiling water over the flour in a bowl and stir quickly with chopsticks or a fork until combined. This hot water helps partially cook the flour, making the dough more elastic and easier to work with. Once cool enough to handle, knead the dough on a floured surface for about 8-10 minutes until smooth and pliable. Rest the dough covered with a damp cloth for at least 30 minutes to relax the gluten, which prevents it from shrinking when you roll it out.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough feels smooth, elastic, and no longer sticky
Step 2
While the dough rests, prepare the filling. Mix ground pork with soy sauce, sesame oil, grated ginger, minced garlic, sugar, and white pepper. Stir well until the mixture becomes sticky; this helps bind the meat and broth together. Next, gently fold in the chilled chicken broth so it’s evenly distributed but the filling remains thick enough to hold its shape. Refrigerate to keep it cold and firm, which is essential for trapping the broth inside the dumplings.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Filling is sticky and cold but not liquidy
Step 3
Divide the rested dough into small equal pieces, about 24. Roll each piece into a thin circle, roughly 3 inches in diameter. It’s important not to make the wrappers too thick; thin wrappers cook faster and let you enjoy the broth without the dough overpowering the bite. Keep the wrappers covered with a damp towel to prevent drying out while you work.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wrappers are thin, flexible, and evenly rolled
Step 4
Place a spoonful of filling in the center of each wrapper. Carefully pleat the edges up to seal the dumpling, making sure there are no gaps or holes where broth might leak out. This step can be tricky, so take your time to pinch the dough tightly. A good seal ensures the broth stays inside and the dumpling holds its shape during cooking.
⏱ Time: 20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dumplings are neatly sealed with no cracks
Step 5
Set up a steamer lined with parchment paper or cabbage leaves to prevent sticking. Arrange dumplings with some space between them and steam over boiling water for about 8 minutes. Steaming cooks the dough evenly and melts the broth inside, but be careful not to over-steam or the wrappers will become soggy and difficult to handle.
⏱ Time: 8 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wrappers turn translucent and filling is cooked through
Step 6
While steaming, heat vegetable oil in a non-stick skillet over medium heat. Once the dumplings are steamed, transfer them carefully to the hot skillet. Fry for about 2-3 minutes until the bottoms turn golden and crispy. This pan-frying step adds a wonderful texture contrast, making the dumplings irresistible. Watch closely so they don’t burn.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bottoms of dumplings are golden brown and crispy
Step 7
Add a splash of water (about 1/4 cup) to the skillet and immediately cover with a lid to steam the dumplings briefly again. This step ensures the tops stay soft and the filling remains juicy. Let them steam for another 2 minutes or until the water evaporates. The combination of steaming and frying creates that signature crispy-bottom yet tender wrapper experience.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Water evaporates, tops remain soft, bottoms stay crispy
Step 8
Remove the lid and drizzle a little rice vinegar over the dumplings for a subtle tangy finish that balances the rich pork filling. Sprinkle chopped green onions on top for freshness and color. Serve immediately with soy sauce or chili oil on the side for dipping. These dumplings are best enjoyed hot to savor the crispy texture and juicy broth.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dumplings are hot, garnished, and ready to eat
Step 9
Clean up and store any leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge. To reheat, pan-fry again lightly or steam briefly to revive the texture without drying out the filling. Avoid microwaving as it can make the dough tough and the broth leak out. Enjoy your crispy soup dumplings within 2 days for the best flavor and texture.
⏱ Time: N/A
✓ Doneness: Leftovers are reheated evenly and remain juicy
