Soft Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise and Citrus Zest

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 7, 2026
20 min Prep Time
15 min Time
24 Servings
Medium Difficulty

These Italian Easter cookies are delightfully soft with a bright citrus zing and a subtle anise flavor. Topped with cheerful sprinkles, they’re a festive treat to share at any spring gathering.

Pro Tips

Don’t skip chilling the dough before baking.
Chilling firms up the butter, which controls cookie spread and enhances flavor by letting the ingredients meld. Baking unchilled dough often results in flat, greasy cookies that lack structure and chew.
Use fresh lemon zest instead of dried or bottled.
Fresh zest contains essential oils that provide vibrant citrus aroma and flavor. Bottled zest or dried peel lacks the brightness and can taste dull, affecting the overall cookie flavor.
Mix the dough just until combined after adding flour.
Overmixing develops gluten, making cookies tough instead of tender. Gentle folding keeps the dough light and produces a soft crumb, which is key for these cookies.
Use unsalted butter at room temperature, not melted.
Room temp butter creams with sugar to trap air, creating a lighter texture. Melted butter leads to greasy dough and overly flat cookies.
Rotate baking sheets halfway through baking.
Ovens often have hot spots; rotating ensures even baking and consistent cookie texture and color across the batch.
Add sprinkles just before baking, not after.
Sprinkles adhere better to the dough and slightly melt on top, creating a pretty, festive look. Adding after baking can cause them to fall off.
Use parchment paper or silicone mats to prevent sticking.
These liners provide a non-stick surface and promote even heat distribution, preventing cookies from burning or sticking, which preserves their delicate texture.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the dough after adding the flour

Why it happens Many cooks assume more mixing means better incorporation, but this develops gluten, making cookies tough and chewy.

Fix Fold the flour in gently with a spatula just until no streaks remain. Stop mixing immediately to preserve tenderness.

Using melted butter instead of softened

Why it happens Some think melted butter is easier to mix, but it changes dough consistency and prevents proper creaming.

Fix Use butter softened to room temperature—not melted or cold. Cream it with sugar until fluffy before adding other ingredients.

Skipping the chilling step

Why it happens Impatience or lack of fridge space can lead cooks to bake dough right away.

Fix Plan ahead to chill dough at least 30 minutes. This controls spread and helps flavors develop.

Not measuring flour correctly

Why it happens Scooping flour directly with a cup can pack it tightly, adding too much flour.

Fix Spoon flour into measuring cup and level off with a knife for accurate measurement.

Baking at too high temperature

Why it happens Rushing baking or inaccurate oven temp leads to burnt edges and dry cookies.

Fix Use an oven thermometer and bake at 350°F (175°C) as directed for even cooking.

Variations

Orange and Anise Italian Easter Cookies

Swap lemon zest and juice for fresh orange zest and juice to give these cookies a sweeter, more rounded citrus flavor. The anise pairs beautifully with orange, adding depth. Expect a slightly softer, more fragrant cookie with a warm citrus aroma.

Note: Orange juice is less acidic than lemon, so the dough may spread a bit more; chilling is even more important here.

Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies

Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend with xanthan gum instead of regular flour. The texture will be a bit more crumbly but still tender. These are perfect for those with gluten sensitivities who want to enjoy traditional flavors.

Note: Gluten-free dough may need a touch more moisture; adjust milk slightly to get the right consistency.

Almond Italian Easter Cookies

Add 1/2 cup finely ground almonds to the flour mixture for a nutty twist. Almonds add moisture and a subtle crunch inside the soft cookie. This variation pairs well with a dusting of powdered sugar instead of sprinkles.

Note: Almonds absorb moisture, so reduce flour by about 1/4 cup to keep dough soft.

Vegan Italian Easter Cookies

Replace butter with coconut oil and eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp flaxseed meal + 3 tbsp water per egg). The texture will be slightly different—more crumbly but still flavorful. Use plant-based milk to keep it vegan-friendly.

Note: Coconut oil solidifies when chilled, so dough may be firmer; handle gently when scooping.

Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies

After baking and cooling, dip half of each cookie into melted dark chocolate and let set. The chocolate adds a rich contrast to the bright citrus and anise flavors, making these cookies extra special for celebrations.

Note: Use high-quality chocolate and allow cookies to cool completely before dipping to prevent melting.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain their soft texture and prevent sprinkles from melting.

Refrigerator

Cookies can be stored in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 7 days. They may firm up and lose a bit of softness but can be warmed slightly before serving to refresh texture.

Freezer

Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet until solid, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving.

Reheating

Warm cookies gently in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes or microwave for 10-15 seconds with a damp paper towel nearby to retain moisture. Avoid overheating, which can dry them out.

Make Ahead

Prepare the dough 1-2 days ahead and keep it tightly wrapped in the fridge. Shape and bake cookies on the day you plan to serve for the freshest flavor and best texture.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with something else?
Yes, if you don’t have anise extract, you can use a small amount of fennel seed or star anise powder, but use sparingly as they’re stronger in flavor. Alternatively, omit it for a milder cookie, but it’s a key note in traditional Italian Easter cookies.
Why are my cookies spreading too much during baking?
This usually happens if the dough is too warm or the butter was melted rather than softened. Chilling the dough before baking helps control spread. Also, make sure your flour is measured correctly and your oven temperature is accurate.
Can I make these cookies without eggs?
Yes, you can substitute eggs with flax eggs or applesauce, but the texture will be slightly different—less chewy and more crumbly. I recommend flax eggs for the closest texture match in vegan versions.
How do I keep the cookies soft for longer?
Store cookies in an airtight container with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel inside to maintain moisture. Avoid refrigerating unless necessary, as cold can dry them out.
What’s the best way to zest lemons for this recipe?
Use a microplane or fine grater to zest only the yellow outer skin, avoiding the bitter white pith underneath. Fresh zest adds bright flavor and aroma that bottled zest can’t match.
Can I use bottled lemon juice instead of fresh?
Fresh lemon juice is best because it has a brighter, fresher flavor and natural acidity that activates the baking powder properly. Bottled juice lacks these qualities and can dull the taste.
Are these cookies supposed to be crunchy or soft?
These Italian Easter cookies are traditionally soft and tender with a slight chew. Overbaking will dry them out, so watch for set edges and soft centers when removing from the oven.
Can I freeze the dough instead of baked cookies?
Yes, you can freeze the shaped dough balls on a tray, then transfer to a bag. Bake straight from frozen, adding a couple extra minutes to the baking time. This is great for prepping ahead.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1/4 cup fresh lemon juice
  • 1 tablespoon lemon zest
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup whole milk
  • Colorful sprinkles for topping

How To Do It

Step 1
In a large bowl, cream together the softened butter and granulated sugar using a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed. This step is crucial because creaming incorporates air into the mixture, which helps give the cookies a lighter texture. Beat until the mixture is pale and fluffy, usually about 3-5 minutes. Avoid rushing this step or using too low speed, as the cookies might turn out dense. You’ll know it’s done when the mixture looks creamy and smooth, with no grainy sugar visible.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Mixture is pale, fluffy, and smooth without visible sugar granules
Step 2
Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition to fully incorporate them. Then mix in the lemon zest, lemon juice, and anise extract. These flavorings give the cookies their signature bright and aromatic notes. If you skip or rush this part, the flavors won’t meld properly, and the dough might separate. Mix just until combined to avoid overworking the dough, which can make the cookies tough.
⏱ Time: 3
✓ Doneness: Smooth, glossy batter with even distribution of zest and liquid ingredients
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. Sifting removes lumps and aerates the flour, which helps with a tender texture. Mixing these dry ingredients thoroughly ensures even leavening and consistent flavor throughout the dough.
⏱ Time: 2
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are evenly combined with no lumps or clumps
Step 4
Gradually add the dry ingredients into the wet mixture in three parts, alternating with the milk in two parts. Start and end with the flour mixture. Use a spatula or low mixer speed to fold gently after each addition. This alternating method prevents the dough from becoming too stiff or overworked. Overmixing here is a common mistake that can lead to dense cookies.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Dough is smooth, slightly sticky, and well combined with no dry patches
Step 5
Once the dough is formed, cover it with plastic wrap and chill in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes. Chilling firms the dough, making it easier to handle and helping the cookies keep their shape during baking. Skipping this step can cause the cookies to spread too much and lose their characteristic soft texture.
⏱ Time: 30
✓ Doneness: Dough is firm to the touch and cold
Step 6
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line baking sheets with parchment paper. Using a tablespoon or cookie scoop, drop dough balls onto the baking sheet, spacing them about 2 inches apart to allow for slight spreading. The dough should be firm but pliable after chilling; if too hard, let it sit for 5 minutes before scooping.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Dough balls hold shape and are evenly spaced on baking sheet
Step 7
Before baking, lightly press the top of each dough ball and sprinkle generously with colorful sprinkles. This not only adds a festive look but also a bit of crunch contrast. The sprinkles should stick nicely without sinking into the dough, which means the dough consistency is just right.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Sprinkles evenly covering cookie tops without melting into dough
Step 8
Bake cookies for 12-15 minutes, rotating the pans halfway through to ensure even baking. The cookies are done when the edges are set but the centers still look slightly soft and puffy. Avoid overbaking, as these cookies are meant to be tender and chewy rather than crisp. They will firm up as they cool.
⏱ Time: 15
✓ Doneness: Edges are lightly golden and cookie centers appear set but soft
Step 9
Remove cookies from oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. Cooling on the sheet allows the cookies to finish setting gently, preventing cracking or drying out. Once cooled, they should feel soft with a delicate crumb. Store in an airtight container to keep them fresh.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Cookies are firm enough to handle but remain soft and tender
