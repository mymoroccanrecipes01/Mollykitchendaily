Hearty Spring Minestrone Soup with Fresh Veggies and Herbs

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 1, 2026
20 min Prep Time
50 min Time
6 Servings
Medium Difficulty

This minestrone soup is packed with fresh spring vegetables and fragrant herbs. The slow-simmered broth brings out deep, comforting flavors. It’s a perfect light dinner or cozy lunch on chilly days.

Pro Tips

Don’t rush the soffritto — cook the onion, carrot, and celery low and slow.
Slow cooking these aromatics allows their natural sugars to caramelize gently, building a sweet, savory base without burning. If you cook too fast or at high heat, you risk bitterness and uneven flavor development, which dulls the soup’s overall taste.
Add the pasta just before the end to keep it al dente.
Pasta absorbs liquid and swells as it cooks. Adding it too early causes mushiness and thickens the broth undesirably. Adding it last ensures it cooks perfectly tender but firm, maintaining a pleasant texture and balanced broth consistency.
Use canned beans but rinse them well before adding.
Rinsing removes excess sodium and starch from the canning liquid that can make the broth cloudy or overly salty. It also freshens the beans’ flavor, preventing any metallic or canned taste and keeping the soup bright and clean.
Simmer the soup gently, don’t boil aggressively.
A gentle simmer allows flavors to meld without breaking down vegetables too much or clouding the broth. Boiling hard can cause veggies to become mushy and the broth to lose clarity and brightness.
Add fresh spinach and basil at the very end.
Adding delicate greens and herbs last preserves their vibrant color and fresh flavor. Cooking them too long makes them wilt excessively and lose their bright taste, which balances the earthy soup.
Taste and adjust salt gradually throughout cooking.
Salt enhances flavor but can easily be overdone. Adding it slowly and tasting often helps you reach perfect seasoning without oversalting, especially as broth reduces and flavors concentrate.
Use vegetable broth with a good flavor base or homemade if possible.
The broth is the soup’s backbone. A bland or watery broth results in a flat soup. Using a flavorful broth enhances every ingredient and results in a richer, more satisfying final dish.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Adding pasta too early

Why it happens Many cooks add pasta with the initial broth to save time, assuming it will cook evenly.

Fix Add pasta in the last 8-10 minutes of cooking to ensure it stays al dente. This prevents mushy texture and keeps the broth from becoming overly thick and starchy.

Burning the garlic

Why it happens Garlic cooks quickly and can burn if left unattended or cooked on high heat.

Fix Add garlic after the soffritto has softened and stir constantly for just 30-60 seconds until fragrant. Remove from heat if necessary to avoid bitterness.

Overcooking fresh vegetables

Why it happens Simmering soup too long or at too high heat breaks down delicate vegetables.

Fix Add tender vegetables like zucchini and spinach later in cooking and maintain a gentle simmer to preserve texture and color.

Not rinsing canned beans

Why it happens Some overlook the extra step thinking canned beans are ready to use.

Fix Drain and rinse beans under cold water to remove excess salt and starch, improving broth clarity and flavor.

Skipping the tasting and seasoning step

Why it happens Some assume the soup will be perfectly seasoned from the start without adjustments.

Fix Taste the soup at least twice during cooking and adjust salt and pepper gradually to balance flavors as they develop.

Variations

Add Sausage for Hearty Flavor

Brown Italian sausage in the pot before the soffritto to infuse the soup with savory richness. This adds protein and a spicy kick, making it more filling for colder days.

Note: Drain excess fat after browning sausage to avoid greasy broth and adjust salt accordingly.

Make It Vegan by Skipping Cheese

Omit Parmesan and use vegetable broth to keep the soup fully plant-based. Add nutritional yeast on serving for a cheesy flavor without dairy.

Note: Ensure broth is vegan and use fresh herbs to enhance umami in absence of cheese.

Use Seasonal Winter Vegetables

Swap spring veggies for root vegetables like parsnips, turnips, and kale for a winter minestrone twist. This changes texture to heartier and flavors to earthier.

Note: Increase simmer time slightly to soften denser root vegetables but still add greens last.

Add Pesto Swirl for Extra Herbiness

Stir a spoonful of basil pesto into each bowl before serving to boost herb flavor and add richness. Works great as a finishing touch for brightening the soup.

Note: Add pesto off heat to preserve its fresh basil flavor and vibrant color.

Use Brown Rice Instead of Pasta

Replace pasta with cooked brown rice for a gluten-free, fiber-rich adaptation. The texture is chewier and adds a nutty flavor dimension.

Note: Add cooked rice at the end just to warm through; avoid cooking rice directly in soup to prevent mushiness.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

This soup can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely. Beyond this, bacteria growth increases rapidly, so refrigerate promptly.

Refrigerator

Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavors meld nicely overnight but pasta may absorb broth and soften further. Reheat gently on the stove with a splash of broth to refresh texture.

Freezer

Freeze in a sealed container for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, leave some headspace and cool completely before freezing. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat slowly to preserve texture.

Reheating

Reheat on the stovetop over medium-low heat, stirring occasionally. Add a splash of broth or water if soup seems too thick. Avoid microwave reheating at high power as it can overcook pasta and wilt vegetables unevenly.

Make Ahead

Prepare the broth and vegetables up to 2 days ahead and refrigerate separately from pasta. Cook pasta fresh just before serving to maintain ideal texture and avoid mushiness.

FAQ

Can I use dried beans instead of canned?
Absolutely! If using dried beans, soak them overnight and cook them fully before adding to the soup. This prevents undercooking and ensures beans are tender. Adjust cooking time accordingly as dried beans need much longer than canned ones.
What if I don’t have vegetable broth?
You can use water with added herbs and a bit of salt, but broth adds essential flavor depth. Alternatively, chicken broth works if you’re not vegetarian, just remember it changes the flavor profile slightly.
How can I make this soup spicier?
Add a pinch of red pepper flakes when sautéing the soffritto or stir in some harissa or hot sauce at the end. Start small and adjust to your heat preference.
Can I freeze the soup with pasta inside?
It’s best to freeze the soup without pasta because pasta can become mushy after thawing. Freeze the broth and vegetables separately, then cook fresh pasta when ready to eat.
Why does my soup get cloudy?
Cloudiness usually comes from starch released by pasta or beans. To minimize this, rinse canned beans well and add pasta last, cooking it just before serving. Avoid boiling vigorously.
Can I make this soup in a slow cooker?
Yes! Add all ingredients except pasta and fresh spinach to the slow cooker and cook on low for 6-8 hours. Add pasta and spinach in the last 30 minutes to prevent overcooking.
What pasta shapes work best?
Small shapes like ditalini, elbow macaroni, or small shells are ideal as they cook quickly and blend well with the soup’s texture. Larger pasta shapes can overpower the dish.
How do I adjust seasoning if soup tastes bland?
Simmer longer to concentrate flavors, then add salt gradually and taste frequently. A squeeze of lemon juice or a splash of vinegar can brighten flavors if needed.

What You'll Need

Approximately 8 cups, 6 servings

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil, extra virgin
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 medium carrots, diced
  • 2 celery stalks, diced
  • 1 medium zucchini, diced
  • 1 cup green beans, trimmed and cut
  • 1 cup fresh spinach, chopped
  • 1 can (14 oz) diced tomatoes with juice
  • 6 cups vegetable broth
  • 1/2 cup small pasta (like ditalini or elbow macaroni)
  • 1 can (15 oz) cannellini beans, drained and rinsed
  • 1 teaspoon dried oregano
  • 1/2 teaspoon dried thyme
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
  • Fresh basil leaves, chopped, for garnish
  • Grated Parmesan cheese, for serving (optional)

How To Do It

Step 1
Heat the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the diced onion, carrot, and celery, stirring occasionally. This base of soffritto develops a sweet, savory aroma that forms the flavor foundation of the soup. Cook until the vegetables soften but don’t brown, about 8-10 minutes. Avoid rushing this step by turning the heat too high, which can burn the onion and introduce bitterness. The softness here helps release natural sugars that deepen the broth later.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Vegetables should be tender and translucent, with a gentle sweetness in their aroma.
Step 2
Add the minced garlic, stirring constantly for about 1 minute until fragrant but not browned. Garlic cooks quickly and can turn bitter if overcooked, so keep a close eye. This step infuses the oil with garlic flavor, which will carry through the whole soup. Once fragrant, add the diced zucchini and green beans, stirring to combine. The fresh vegetables add crunch and brightness, so don’t overcook them here.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Garlic aroma should be noticeable but not sharp; veggies slightly softened but still vibrant.
Step 3
Pour in the canned diced tomatoes with their juices and the vegetable broth. Stir well to combine all ingredients. The acid from the tomatoes brightens the broth and balances the sweetness of the soffritto. Bring the soup to a gentle boil, then reduce heat to low and simmer uncovered. Simmering slowly allows flavors to meld and the broth to deepen gradually. Avoid boiling aggressively, which can break down the vegetables too much.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth should be flavorful, slightly thickened, and vegetables tender but intact.
Step 4
Add the dried oregano, thyme, salt, and pepper to the simmering soup. Season gradually and taste as you go to avoid over-salting. Herbs add complexity and a subtle earthiness that complements the fresh vegetables. Stir well and continue simmering. This step layers flavor and helps the herbs infuse their oils into the broth.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Herbal aroma should be noticeable, seasoning balanced but not overpowering.
Step 5
Add the rinsed cannellini beans to the pot. Beans add protein and creaminess, making the soup more filling without heaviness. Stir gently to avoid mashing the beans. Simmer for 5 minutes to warm them through and allow them to blend with the broth flavors.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Beans warmed and intact, broth slightly thickened.
Step 6
Add the small pasta to the soup and cook until al dente, following package instructions (usually 7-9 minutes). Adding pasta last prevents it from getting mushy and soaking up too much broth. Stir occasionally to stop pasta from sticking to the bottom. Timing is key here; undercooked pasta will be hard, overcooked will be mushy and ruin the soup's texture.
⏱ Time: 8 minutes
✓ Doneness: Pasta should be tender but firm to bite, evenly cooked.
Step 7
Stir in the chopped fresh spinach just before turning off the heat. Spinach wilts quickly, adding color and freshness without losing texture. Adding it last keeps it vibrant and prevents overcooking, which can make it mushy and dull the flavor.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Spinach bright green and just wilted.
Step 8
Taste the soup and adjust seasoning with more salt or pepper if needed. This final seasoning check ensures the flavors are balanced after all ingredients have mingled. Don’t skip this step because simmering can mellow spices and saltiness.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flavors balanced, broth savory with a hint of brightness.
Step 9
Serve hot, garnished with chopped fresh basil and a sprinkle of grated Parmesan cheese if you like. The fresh basil adds a lovely aromatic finish, while Parmesan brings a salty richness that complements the soup beautifully. If serving later, cool the soup before refrigerating to preserve flavor and texture.
⏱ Time: Immediate serving
✓ Doneness: Soup steaming hot with fresh herb aroma and cheesy garnish.
