Silky Spring Pea Soup with Fresh Mint for a Light, Bright Lunch

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 3, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This spring pea soup is smooth and bright with a hint of mint, bringing fresh flavors to your table. It’s quick to make and perfect when you want a light, comforting starter or lunch that feels like a spring day in a bowl.

Pro Tips

Use an immersion blender directly in the pot for easy pureeing.
Immersion blenders let you puree the soup without transferring it to a blender, saving time and reducing mess. It also allows you to control texture more precisely, blending just until smooth. Avoid over-blending as it can heat the soup too much or cause it to become gummy.
Add cream off the heat to keep it from curdling.
Cream curdles if boiled or heated too rapidly. Adding it after removing the soup from heat ensures it blends smoothly, giving a rich texture without lumps. This simple step maintains the soup’s silky mouthfeel.
Sauté onions slowly over medium-low heat to develop sweetness.
Slow cooking onions releases their natural sugars gently, adding a subtle sweetness that complements the peas. High heat can burn them, creating bitterness. Patience here pays off in flavor depth.
Use fresh peas when possible for the best flavor and texture.
Fresh peas have a sweeter, more tender bite than frozen. They also retain a brighter color during cooking, making the soup visually appealing. Frozen peas are convenient but can be slightly less vibrant.
Add the mint leaves after cooking to preserve their fresh flavor.
Cooking mint too long diminishes its bright flavor and can make it taste bitter. Adding it at the end allows the essential oils to infuse the soup without losing freshness.
Season gradually and taste often, especially when adding lemon juice.
Lemon juice brightens but can easily overpower if added too much. Tasting as you go helps balance acidity and salt, ensuring the soup is flavorful but not harsh.
Serve with crusty bread to add texture and make it a satisfying meal.
The creamy soup is light on its own, so pairing it with bread adds a contrasting crunch and makes the meal more filling and enjoyable.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overcooking peas until dull and mushy

Why it happens People often boil soup too vigorously or too long, thinking it will speed cooking, but this causes peas to lose their bright color and fresh flavor.

Fix Simmer the soup gently and check peas early for tenderness. Remove from heat as soon as peas are soft but still vibrant green.

Adding cream while soup is boiling

Why it happens Some assume cream can be added anytime, but boiling cream causes it to curdle and separate.

Fix Stir cream in after removing soup from heat, gently warming it without boiling to keep texture smooth.

Not pureeing soup enough

Why it happens In a rush, some skip thorough blending, leaving chunks or grainy texture that feels less refined.

Fix Use a good blender or immersion blender to puree until fully smooth for that signature silky feel.

Using dried mint instead of fresh

Why it happens Dried herbs are convenient, but dried mint lacks the bright, fresh flavor needed here.

Fix Always use fresh mint leaves and add them at the end of cooking to preserve flavor.

Skipping lemon juice

Why it happens Sometimes lemon is forgotten or thought unnecessary, but it really lifts the soup’s flavor.

Fix Add a small squeeze of fresh lemon juice at the end and adjust to taste to brighten the overall dish.

Variations

Dairy-Free Coconut Pea Soup

Swap heavy cream for coconut cream to make the soup vegan and dairy-free. This adds a subtle tropical sweetness that pairs nicely with peas and mint. The texture remains creamy but with a slight coconut flavor that changes the profile pleasantly.

Note: Use full-fat coconut cream and add it off heat, just like dairy cream, to avoid curdling or separation.

Add Fresh Asparagus for Extra Veggies

Include chopped fresh asparagus with the peas for more texture and a deeper spring flavor. Cook asparagus with peas until tender before blending. This variation gives the soup a more complex vegetable profile and a bit more bite.

Note: Trim asparagus and cut into small pieces to cook evenly with peas during simmering.

Spicy Spring Pea Soup

Add a pinch of cayenne pepper or a diced jalapeño during the onion sauté for a subtle spicy kick. The heat contrasts nicely with the sweet peas and fresh mint, giving the soup a fun twist for spice lovers.

Note: Adjust heat level carefully to avoid overpowering the delicate pea flavor.

Herbed Pea Soup with Basil and Tarragon

Mix fresh basil and tarragon leaves with mint for an aromatic herb blend. This variation adds complexity and a slightly sweet, anise-like flavor that complements the peas beautifully.

Note: Add herbs after cooking as with mint to maintain their fresh flavors.

Pea and Potato Soup for Creamier Texture

Add peeled, diced potato to the pot with onions and garlic. Potatoes add body and creaminess when pureed, making the soup heartier while still maintaining a silky texture.

Note: Simmer potatoes fully with broth before adding peas to ensure even cooking and smooth texture.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

This soup can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered. Beyond that, bacteria growth increases, so refrigerate promptly. Covering with a lid or plastic wrap helps prevent contamination and drying.

Refrigerator

Store leftover soup in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. The soup thickens when cold; reheat gently on the stove or microwave with a splash of broth or water to loosen it. Flavor may mellow when cold, so adjust seasoning after reheating.

Freezer

Freeze soup in airtight containers or heavy-duty freezer bags for up to 3 months. To avoid freezer burn, remove excess air and wrap containers well. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating gently on stove or microwave.

Reheating

Reheat over low heat on the stovetop, stirring occasionally, adding a splash of broth or water if too thick. Avoid boiling after adding cream to prevent curdling. Microwave reheating in short bursts with stirring works too.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the soup base (onions, garlic, broth, peas) 1-2 days ahead and refrigerate. Add mint, blend, and stir in cream just before serving to keep flavors fresh and texture perfect.

FAQ

Can I use frozen peas instead of fresh?
Yes, frozen peas work well and are often picked at peak freshness, locking in flavor. Just reduce cooking time slightly as frozen peas cook faster. The soup may be a touch less vibrant but still delicious.
How do I make this soup vegan?
Simply swap the heavy cream for full-fat coconut cream or another plant-based cream alternative. Add it off heat just as you would dairy cream to keep the texture smooth and creamy.
What if I don’t have fresh mint?
Fresh mint is best for flavor and brightness. If unavailable, you can omit it or try fresh basil for a different herbal note. Dried mint isn’t recommended as it can taste bitter and lacks freshness.
Can I prepare this soup in advance?
Yes, you can cook and blend the soup base a day ahead. Store it refrigerated without cream and mint, then add those fresh just before serving to preserve flavor and texture.
Why is my soup grainy after blending?
This usually happens if the soup isn’t pureed long enough or the blender isn’t powerful enough. Blend longer or use an immersion blender for smoother texture. Straining can also help if needed.
How do I store leftover soup?
Keep leftovers in airtight containers in the fridge for up to 3 days or freeze for up to 3 months. Reheat gently with a splash of broth to restore texture.
Can I add other herbs to this soup?
Definitely! Basil, tarragon, or parsley work well with peas and mint. Add fresh herbs after cooking to keep their flavors bright.
What bread pairs best with this soup?
Crusty baguette, sourdough, or whole grain bread are great choices. They add texture and soak up the creamy soup nicely.

What You'll Need

Approximately 4 servings

  • 4 cups fresh or frozen spring peas
  • 1 small onion, chopped
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 3 cups vegetable broth
  • 1/2 cup fresh mint leaves, loosely packed
  • 1/2 cup heavy cream or coconut cream
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Juice of half a lemon

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by heating the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and cook gently for about 5 minutes until it softens but doesn’t brown — this slow cooking releases the onion’s sweetness, which forms a gentle base flavor for your soup. Avoid rushing this step by turning up the heat, or the onions will brown and create a bitter taste that overshadows the peas.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Onions are translucent and soft but not browned or caramelized
Step 2
Add the minced garlic to the softened onions and cook for another 1-2 minutes. Garlic cooks quickly and can burn easily, so stir frequently and keep the heat moderate. You want the garlic fragrant and soft to deepen the soup's flavor without bitterness. Burnt garlic will cause an unpleasant sharpness in the final dish.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Garlic aroma is fragrant and gentle, no dark spots on garlic
Step 3
Pour in the vegetable broth and bring it to a gentle boil. This liquid is the backbone of your soup, so using a flavorful broth is key. Once boiling, reduce heat to a simmer and add the peas. Simmering gently helps preserve the peas’ bright color and fresh flavor. Boiling too hard or too long will dull their vibrancy and turn the soup dull.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Peas are tender and bright green, broth is simmering gently
Step 4
Remove the pot from heat and add the fresh mint leaves. Using fresh mint rather than dried gives the soup a lively, herbaceous note. Let the mint steep in the hot broth for a few minutes to release its oils before blending. If you add mint too early or cook it, the flavor will fade and become flat.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mint leaves are wilted but still vibrant green, aroma is fresh and minty
Step 5
Using an immersion blender or transferring to a blender in batches, puree the soup until completely smooth. This step is essential for achieving that silky texture. Pureeing thoroughly breaks down the peas and onions into a creamy consistency without needing extra thickening agents. If you don’t blend enough, the soup will be grainy or chunky, which changes the mouthfeel.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is smooth with no visible pea skins or chunks
Step 6
Return the pureed soup to low heat and stir in the cream slowly, warming gently but not boiling. Adding cream off high heat prevents it from curdling and keeps the texture luscious. If you rush this or bring it to a boil, the cream might separate, ruining the smoothness.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is warm, creamy, and velvety without separation or curdling
Step 7
Season the soup with salt, pepper, and a squeeze of fresh lemon juice to brighten the flavors. Lemon juice cuts through the richness and highlights the peas’ natural sweetness. Taste as you go — adding too much salt or lemon can overpower the delicate balance, so add gradually.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flavors are balanced, fresh, and bright without harshness
Step 8
Serve the soup hot with a garnish of fresh mint leaves and a drizzle of olive oil if you like. The garnish adds a fresh herbal note and a beautiful pop of color. This final touch makes the soup feel special and inviting.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Soup looks vibrant green with fresh mint garnish
Step 9
Pair your spring pea soup with crusty bread or a light salad for a complete meal. The soup is light but filling enough when combined with bread. This pairing balances textures and adds a satisfying element to the meal.
⏱ Time: 0 minutes
✓ Doneness: Meal is balanced with soup and complementary sides
