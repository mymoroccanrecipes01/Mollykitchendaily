Fluffy British Cheddar Biscuits That Melt in Your Mouth

Molly Kitchen Daily
Published on April 22, 2026
15 min Prep Time
20 min Time
8 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These British cheddar biscuits are soft, buttery, and packed with sharp cheese flavor. The secret? Folding the dough gently to keep them fluffy. Great for cozy snacks or paired with your favorite soup!

Pro Tips

Keep your butter and milk cold until just before mixing.
Cold butter creates steam pockets during baking that puff up the biscuits. If the butter melts before baking, you lose those pockets, resulting in dense biscuits. Similarly, cold milk helps keep the butter solid while mixing, preserving the flaky texture.
Don’t overmix your dough—fold gently until just combined.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which can make biscuits tough and chewy instead of tender. Gentle folding preserves the delicate layers and lets the biscuits rise properly.
Use a sharp biscuit cutter and press straight down without twisting.
Twisting the cutter seals the dough edges and prevents the biscuits from rising evenly. Pressing straight down cuts clean edges, allowing the biscuits to rise tall and fluffy.
Bake biscuits close together on the baking sheet.
Placing biscuits close allows them to support each other’s rise, keeping them tall and soft. If spaced too far apart, they spread out and become flatter.
Rotate the baking sheet halfway through baking.
Ovens often have hot spots causing uneven browning. Rotating the pan ensures all biscuits bake evenly to a golden brown without burning on one side.
Let biscuits cool slightly before serving.
Cooling lets steam inside settle so the texture firms up without becoming gummy. Biscuits eaten straight from the oven are delicious but can be fragile and crumbly.
Use fresh baking powder for best rise.
Old baking powder loses potency, leading to flat biscuits. Fresh baking powder ensures a strong, consistent rise for fluffy results.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using warm butter instead of cold

Why it happens Many assume softened butter is best for mixing, but warm butter melts into the flour instead of creating steam pockets.

Fix Chill the butter thoroughly before cutting into the flour. If it softens too much, pop it back in the fridge for a bit to firm up again before continuing.

Overmixing the dough

Why it happens In a rush to combine ingredients, it’s easy to mix too much, developing gluten that toughens biscuits.

Fix Mix until just combined and no dry flour remains. Use gentle folding with a spatula instead of vigorous stirring.

Twisting the biscuit cutter

Why it happens Twisting is a natural instinct to cut through dough, but it seals edges and prevents proper rise.

Fix Press the cutter straight down and lift straight up without twisting to keep edges clean.

Not preheating the oven fully

Why it happens Impatience or distraction can cause baking in a not-hot-enough oven, leading to poor rise and pale biscuits.

Fix Always preheat the oven completely to 425°F before baking. Use an oven thermometer if unsure.

Using pre-shredded cheese

Why it happens Convenience leads many to use pre-shredded cheese, but it contains additives that affect melting.

Fix Grate your own sharp cheddar fresh for smoother melting and better flavor.

Variations

Herb and Garlic Cheddar Biscuits

Add 1 tablespoon of finely chopped fresh herbs like rosemary or thyme and 1 teaspoon of garlic powder to the dry ingredients. This gives the biscuits a fragrant, savory twist that pairs wonderfully with soups and stews.

Note: Be careful not to add too much moisture from fresh herbs; chop finely and pat dry if needed to keep dough consistency.

Buttermilk Cheddar Biscuits

Swap the whole milk for the same amount of cold buttermilk. This adds a tangy flavor and tender crumb thanks to the acidity reacting with baking powder, giving a slightly fluffier texture.

Note: Buttermilk may cause a slightly darker crust due to higher acidity, so watch baking time carefully.

Spicy Cheddar Biscuits

Add 1/2 teaspoon cayenne pepper and a pinch of smoked paprika to the dry ingredients for a smoky, spicy kick. Great for pairing with chili or barbecue dishes.

Note: Adjust spice levels to taste; too much cayenne can overpower the cheddar flavor.

Cheddar and Bacon Biscuits

Fold in 1/2 cup cooked, crumbled bacon with the cheese for a smoky, meaty addition. These are perfect for breakfast or brunch.

Note: Drain bacon well to avoid adding excess grease, which can affect dough texture.

Vegan Cheddar-Style Biscuits

Use vegan butter and a plant-based milk like oat milk. Substitute cheddar with a sharp vegan cheese shreds that melt well. These biscuits keep the buttery, cheesy feel without dairy.

Note: Vegan cheeses vary in melt quality; test your brand first to ensure good melting.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Biscuits can safely sit at room temperature for up to 4 hours if covered loosely with a clean towel to prevent drying out. For best texture, consume within this time if not refrigerated.

Refrigerator

Store cooled biscuits in an airtight container for up to 3 days. They may become slightly denser when cold but reheat well. Warm them briefly in the oven or microwave before serving to restore softness.

Freezer

Freeze biscuits individually wrapped in plastic wrap and stored in a freezer bag for up to 2 months. Thaw at room temperature or warm directly from frozen in a 350°F oven for 10-12 minutes to refresh texture.

Reheating

Reheat biscuits in a preheated 350°F oven for 8-10 minutes, wrapped in foil to prevent drying. Avoid microwaving too long as it can make them rubbery. Adding a small pat of butter before warming enhances flavor and moisture.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the biscuit dough 1 day ahead, shaped and cut on the baking sheet, covered tightly with plastic wrap and refrigerated. Bake fresh the next day, adding a couple minutes to baking time if cold from fridge.

FAQ

Can I use self-rising flour instead of all-purpose flour?
Using self-rising flour is possible but you should omit the baking powder and salt from the recipe since self-rising flour already contains leavening and salt. The texture might be slightly different, often a bit softer and less flaky.
How do I make these biscuits dairy-free?
Substitute the butter with a vegan butter alternative that’s solid and cold, and swap the milk with plant-based milk like oat or almond milk. Use a dairy-free cheese that melts well if you want to keep the cheesy flavor.
Why are my biscuits dense and not fluffy?
Common causes include overmixing the dough, using warm butter, or not preheating the oven properly. Make sure to keep butter cold, mix gently, and bake at a high temperature for best rise.
Can I freeze the dough before baking?
Yes! You can freeze the shaped biscuits on the baking sheet until solid, then transfer to a freezer bag. Bake from frozen, adding a few extra minutes to the baking time.
What’s the best way to grate cheese for these biscuits?
Use a box grater or microplane to grate the cheese freshly. Avoid pre-shredded cheese since it often contains anti-caking agents that prevent smooth melting.
Can I add herbs or spices to the biscuits?
Absolutely! Fresh or dried herbs like thyme, rosemary, or chives work well. Garlic powder or smoked paprika add great flavor. Just fold them into the dry ingredients.
How do I store leftover baked biscuits?
Cool completely, then store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or in the fridge for 3 days. Reheat gently to restore softness.
Can I use sharp cheddar from different countries?
Yes, but the flavor and melting quality can vary. British or Irish cheddars tend to be sharper and creamier, while some American cheddars can be milder or waxier.

What You'll Need

Approximately 8 biscuits

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon garlic powder (optional)
  • 1/4 teaspoon cayenne pepper (optional)
  • 6 tablespoons unsalted butter, cold and cubed
  • 1 1/4 cups sharp cheddar cheese, grated
  • 3/4 cup whole milk, cold

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 425°F (220°C). This high temperature is key to getting a nice rise and a golden crust on your biscuits. Line a baking sheet with parchment paper and set aside. Preheating the oven fully ensures the biscuits start baking immediately with steam inside, which helps them puff up.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Oven fully heated to 425°F, baking sheet ready
Step 2
In a large bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, salt, garlic powder, and cayenne pepper if using. These dry ingredients set the foundation for the biscuit’s texture and flavor. Sifting helps aerate the flour for lighter biscuits and evenly distributes the baking powder, so you don’t get uneven rises.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients well combined and lump-free
Step 3
Add the cold, cubed butter to the dry ingredients. Using a pastry cutter or your fingertips, cut the butter into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with some pea-sized pieces of butter still visible. These bits of butter melt during baking, creating steam pockets that make the biscuits flaky and tender. Avoid warming the butter too much or overmixing here to keep the texture light.
⏱ Time: 7 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture looks crumbly with pea-sized butter chunks
Step 4
Fold in the grated sharp cheddar cheese evenly, taking care not to overwork the dough. The cheese should be well distributed but not melted or clumped. This gives bursts of cheesy flavor throughout each biscuit. Using a sharp cheddar adds a nice tang and depth that mild cheeses can’t match.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cheese evenly folded in without clumps
Step 5
Pour the cold milk into the flour mixture. Using a spatula, gently fold the milk into the dry ingredients until just combined. The dough will be sticky but cohesive. The cold milk helps keep the butter from melting prematurely and contributes to the biscuits’ tender crumb. Overmixing here can develop gluten, making biscuits tough, so fold until you see no dry flour.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Sticky dough formed with no dry flour patches
Step 6
Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Gently pat it into a rectangle about 3/4 inch thick. Fold the dough over itself 2-3 times, then gently pat it back down. This folding technique creates layers that puff up when baked, giving you those flaky, tender biscuits. Be gentle to avoid overworking the dough.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough feels soft, layered, and about 3/4 inch thick
Step 7
Using a floured biscuit cutter or a glass, cut out biscuits by pressing straight down—avoid twisting, which can seal the edges and prevent rising. Place biscuits close together on the prepared baking sheet to encourage rising upwards instead of spreading out. Gather scraps, gently reshape, and cut more biscuits until all dough is used.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Biscuits cut cleanly with sharp edges, arranged on sheet
Step 8
Bake the biscuits in the preheated oven for 15-20 minutes, rotating the pan halfway through for even browning. You’re aiming for golden brown tops and a flaky texture inside. The high heat helps the butter steam quickly, creating lift and a tender crumb. Avoid opening the oven too often, or the heat will drop and affect rising.
⏱ Time: 15-20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Golden brown tops and firm but tender texture when touched
Step 9
Remove the biscuits from the oven and let them cool slightly on a wire rack. They’re best served warm with butter or alongside soups and stews. Cooling lets the steam inside settle, so the texture isn’t gummy. These biscuits also reheat well, but fresh from the oven is unbeatable.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Biscuits warm, slightly cooled, and ready to enjoy
