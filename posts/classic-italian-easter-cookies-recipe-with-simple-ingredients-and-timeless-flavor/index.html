Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise Flavor

Published on April 7, 2026
15 min Prep Time
20 min Time
24 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These Italian Easter cookies are light, subtly sweet, and flavored with anise, making them a perfect spring treat. Simple to make and great for sharing during family gatherings or festive brunches!

Pro Tips

Always sift your dry ingredients before mixing.
Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes baking powder and salt, which prevents clumps and ensures consistent texture and rise. Skipping this can cause uneven baking and dense spots in your cookies.
Chill the dough before baking.
Chilling solidifies the butter, which slows spreading and helps maintain cookie shape. It also allows flavors to meld, improving aroma and taste. Baking unchilled dough often leads to flat, greasy cookies.
Use room temperature eggs for better emulsion.
Room temperature eggs mix more easily with butter and sugar, creating a smooth, stable batter. Cold eggs can cause the mixture to seize or curdle, affecting texture and rise.
Lightly crush anise seeds just before use.
Crushing releases essential oils, enhancing flavor without overpowering. Using whole seeds or powder can result in uneven taste or bitterness.
Don’t overbake the cookies.
Overbaking dries out the cookies and reduces their delicate texture. Watch for light golden edges while the centers remain pale for the perfect balance.
Cool cookies on the baking sheet briefly before transferring.
This allows cookies to firm up and prevents them from breaking apart when moved. Moving them too soon can cause them to crumble.
Use parchment paper or silicone mats for baking.
These liners prevent sticking and promote even heat distribution, resulting in uniform browning and easier cleanup.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Adding eggs too quickly

Why it happens People often try to speed up mixing by adding eggs all at once, not realizing it can cause the batter to curdle or separate.

Fix Add eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition. This ensures proper emulsification and a smooth dough, preventing texture issues.

Skipping the chilling step

Why it happens Impatience or underestimating its importance leads some to skip chilling, thinking it’s unnecessary.

Fix Always chill dough for at least 30 minutes. This solidifies fats and helps maintain cookie shape and flavor during baking.

Overbaking the cookies

Why it happens Not watching the oven closely or baking at too high a temperature can cause dryness.

Fix Remove cookies when edges are lightly golden but centers are still pale. They continue to firm as they cool.

Using melted or cold butter

Why it happens Misunderstanding the butter temperature needed for creaming leads to dense or greasy cookies.

Fix Use butter softened to room temperature, firm but pliable, to trap air during creaming and achieve tender texture.

Not crushing anise seeds

Why it happens Some assume whole seeds are fine as-is or use powder which can be bitter.

Fix Lightly crush seeds before adding to release flavor oils without overpowering the dough.

Variations

Lemon Zest Anise Cookies

Add 1 tablespoon of finely grated lemon zest to the dough for a bright citrus twist that complements the anise beautifully. This variation adds freshness and a slightly tangy aroma, perfect for springtime treats.

Note: Add zest with the wet ingredients to distribute flavor evenly. Baking time remains the same.

Almond Anise Cookies

Incorporate 1/2 cup finely ground almonds into the flour mixture for a nutty flavor and slightly denser texture. These cookies have a lovely chew and a richer taste, ideal for almond lovers.

Note: Reduce flour by 1/2 cup to balance added almonds. Dough may be slightly thicker.

Glazed Anise Cookies

Drizzle a simple glaze made from powdered sugar and lemon juice over cooled cookies for extra sweetness and a shiny finish. This adds a beautiful touch for gifting or special occasions.

Note: Prepare glaze after cookies fully cool to avoid melting.

Anise and Orange Blossom Cookies

Add 1 teaspoon orange blossom water to the wet ingredients for a floral aroma that pairs wonderfully with the anise. This subtle flavor variation makes the cookies feel more festive and exotic.

Note: Reduce any other liquid slightly to compensate. Use sparingly to avoid overpowering.

Chocolate Drizzled Anise Cookies

Once cooled, drizzle melted dark chocolate over the cookies for a rich contrast to the anise flavor. The combination of bittersweet chocolate and aromatic anise is irresistible.

Note: Allow chocolate to set at room temperature before storing.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

These cookies can be stored at room temperature in an airtight container for up to 3 days. Keep them away from heat or humidity to maintain crispness. Cover tightly to avoid drying out or absorbing odors.

Refrigerator

Store cookies in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 7 days. The texture may become slightly softer when chilled, so bring to room temperature before serving for best flavor and crunch.

Freezer

Freeze the cookies in a sealed container or freezer bag for up to 3 months. Separate layers with parchment paper to prevent sticking. Thaw at room temperature for a few hours before eating to restore texture.

Reheating

Warm cookies gently in a 300°F (150°C) oven for 5-7 minutes to refresh their crispness. Avoid microwave reheating as it can make them chewy or rubbery. No additional liquid is needed.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the dough 1-2 days ahead and keep it wrapped in the fridge. This resting time enhances flavor. Bake fresh on the day you plan to serve for best texture and aroma.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract for the seeds?
Yes, you can use about 1 teaspoon of anise extract instead of seeds. However, the flavor will be more concentrated and less textured. I prefer seeds for a fresher, more natural aroma. Add extract with the wet ingredients and reduce or omit any other liquid accordingly.
Why did my cookies spread too much?
Overly soft or melted butter and skipping the chilling step are common causes. Chilling firms the dough and prevents excess spreading. Also, ensure your oven is fully preheated to set the cookie edges quickly.
Can I make these cookies gluten-free?
You can try substituting a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend that includes xanthan gum. Texture may vary slightly, becoming more crumbly. I recommend testing a small batch first and adjusting moisture if needed.
How long do these cookies stay fresh?
Stored properly in an airtight container at room temperature, they stay fresh for about 3 days. Refrigerating extends freshness to about a week but softens texture slightly. Freezing is best for longer storage.
What’s the best way to crush anise seeds?
Use a mortar and pestle or the bottom of a heavy pan to gently crush the seeds. You want to release oils without pulverizing them into powder, which can taste bitter.
Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
Yes, but reduce or omit added salt in the recipe to avoid over-salting. Unsalted butter gives you more control over seasoning, which is why it’s preferred.
My cookies come out dry. What did I do wrong?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or too much flour. Measure flour carefully by spooning and leveling rather than scooping. Also, watch baking time closely and remove cookies when edges just start to brown.
Can I add other spices like cinnamon?
Absolutely! Adding 1/2 teaspoon of cinnamon or cardamom can add warmth and complexity. Just be sure to balance flavors so the anise doesn’t get overshadowed.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies, serves 4-6

  • 2 1/4 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 tablespoon anise seeds, lightly crushed
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • Powdered sugar for dusting

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by creaming the softened butter and sugar together in a large bowl using a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed. This process helps to incorporate air into the mixture, which will make your cookies lighter and give them a tender crumb. Beat until the mixture looks pale and fluffy, about 3-5 minutes. Avoid rushing this step or under-creaming because it can lead to dense cookies that don’t have that melt-in-your-mouth texture.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugar mixture should be noticeably lighter in color and fluffy in texture.
Step 2
Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition. This helps the eggs fully incorporate and emulsify with the butter mixture, ensuring your dough binds well. If you add eggs too quickly or all at once, the mixture can curdle and your dough might separate. After the eggs are fully mixed, stir in the crushed anise seeds gently with a spatula to distribute the flavor evenly.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture should be smooth and glossy, with an even distribution of anise seeds.
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. This step is important to evenly distribute the leavening agent and salt throughout the flour, which will help your cookies rise uniformly and taste balanced. Skipping sifting can cause lumps or uneven mixing, resulting in inconsistent texture.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are fully combined and light with no clumps.
Step 4
Gradually add the dry ingredients into the wet mixture in batches, folding gently with a spatula. Mixing just until combined prevents overworking the gluten in the flour, which could make cookies tough. Be patient and avoid vigorous stirring here to keep the dough tender.
⏱ Time: 4 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough holds together smoothly without streaks of dry flour.
Step 5
Cover the dough with plastic wrap and chill in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes. Chilling firms up the dough, making it easier to handle and preventing the cookies from spreading too much during baking. It also helps the flavors meld together better. Skipping this step can lead to flat, greasy cookies.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough is firm to the touch and slightly cold.
Step 6
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line baking sheets with parchment paper or silicone mats. This ensures even baking and easy cookie removal without sticking. Using parchment also helps prevent overbrowning on the bottom.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes (oven preheat)
✓ Doneness: Oven reaches stable 350°F temperature.
Step 7
Scoop tablespoons of dough and roll into balls. Place them spaced about 2 inches apart on the prepared baking sheets. The spacing is crucial because the cookies will spread slightly, and crowding them will cause them to bake into one another.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Uniform dough balls evenly spaced on sheet.
Step 8
Bake the cookies for 15-20 minutes until the edges turn a light golden brown but the centers remain pale. This ensures a crisp edge with a slightly softer center. Overbaking will dry them out, so keep a close eye toward the end.
⏱ Time: 15-20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are golden and firm, centers look set but not browned.
Step 9
Remove cookies from oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. Cooling on the sheet allows cookies to firm up without breaking. Once fully cool, dust with powdered sugar for a delicate finish that adds subtle sweetness and a pretty look.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies are cool to the touch and hold their shape.
