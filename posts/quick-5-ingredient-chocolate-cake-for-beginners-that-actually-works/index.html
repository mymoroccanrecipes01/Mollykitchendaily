Simple 5-Ingredient Chocolate Cake That Feels Like a Hug in Every Bite

Pro Tips

Always use room temperature eggs and sour cream for better mixing.
Cold ingredients can cause the batter to seize or mix unevenly, leading to lumps or a dense texture. Room temperature ingredients combine more smoothly, producing a uniform batter and ensuring even baking.
Sift your dry ingredients before mixing.
Sifting aerates the flour and cocoa powder, removes lumps, and helps ingredients blend evenly. This results in a lighter, fluffier cake with a fine crumb and consistent texture.
Don’t overmix the batter once wet and dry ingredients are combined.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, making the cake tough and chewy instead of tender. Stir just until no dry flour is visible to keep the crumb soft and delicate.
Use boiling water to bloom the cocoa powder.
Hot water releases the cocoa’s full flavor and creates a thinner batter, which yields a moist cake. Skipping this step results in a dull chocolate taste and denser texture.
Grease and flour your cake pan well before baking.
Properly preparing the pan prevents sticking and ensures the cake releases cleanly, preserving its shape and texture. Using parchment paper on the bottom adds extra insurance.
Check the cake a few minutes before the timer ends.
Ovens vary, so testing early prevents overbaking, which dries out the cake. A toothpick with moist crumbs indicates perfect doneness.
Cool the cake on a wire rack after removing from the pan.
Cooling on a rack allows air circulation around the cake, preventing sogginess and preserving texture. Leaving it in the pan traps steam and softens the bottom too much.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the batter

Why it happens Many cooks assume more mixing means better combined ingredients, but it actually develops gluten and toughens the cake.

Fix Mix ingredients just until combined with no visible dry flour. Use a spatula to fold gently and stop as soon as the batter is uniform but still slightly lumpy.

Skipping the boiling water step

Why it happens Some think adding hot water is unnecessary or risky, but it’s essential for flavor and texture.

Fix Always add boiling water last and stir gently. This blooms the cocoa for richer flavor and thins the batter for moistness.

Using cold ingredients

Why it happens It’s easy to forget to take eggs and sour cream out ahead of time, but cold ingredients mix unevenly.

Fix Bring eggs and sour cream to room temperature before mixing to ensure smooth batter and even baking.

Not greasing the pan well

Why it happens Rushing or skipping this step causes the cake to stick and break apart when removed.

Fix Generously grease and flour the pan, or line with parchment paper for easy release.

Overbaking the cake

Why it happens Waiting too long to check or relying solely on timer leads to drying out the cake.

Fix Start checking for doneness 5 minutes before the timer ends using a toothpick. Pull cake out when a few moist crumbs cling to it.

Variations

Chocolate Cake with Espresso Boost

Add 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder to the dry ingredients to enhance the chocolate flavor with a subtle coffee note. This deepens richness without overpowering the cake.

Note: Espresso powder dissolves easily in the boiling water step, so add it with the dry ingredients for even distribution.

Gluten-Free Chocolate Cake

Replace all-purpose flour with a gluten-free baking blend that includes xanthan gum. The sour cream keeps moisture intact, but expect a slightly different crumb texture.

Note: Baking time may vary; test with a toothpick and expect a slightly denser cake.

Vegan Chocolate Cake

Swap the egg for a flax egg (1 tbsp ground flaxseed + 3 tbsp water) and use dairy-free sour cream. The texture will be slightly different but still moist and delicious.

Note: Let the flax egg sit for 5 minutes to thicken before mixing. Baking time may increase by a few minutes.

Chocolate Cake with Orange Zest

Add 1 tablespoon of fresh orange zest to the batter for a bright citrus twist that pairs beautifully with the chocolate’s richness.

Note: Mix zest with dry ingredients to distribute flavor evenly.

Chocolate Cake with Chocolate Chips

Fold in 1/2 cup semi-sweet chocolate chips into the batter before baking for pockets of melty chocolate throughout the cake.

Note: Coat chips lightly with flour before folding in to prevent sinking.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the cake covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Keep it wrapped tightly with plastic wrap or in a cake dome to prevent it from drying out. Avoid leaving it uncovered as it will quickly lose moisture.

Refrigerator

Keep the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 5 days. The texture may firm up slightly when cold; bring to room temperature before serving or warm gently for best flavor.

Freezer

Freeze the cooled cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and then foil for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature wrapped to retain moisture. Avoid refreezing.

Reheating

Warm slices gently in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (300°F) for 5-10 minutes. Adding a small splash of milk or covering loosely with foil helps retain moisture during reheating.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the batter a few hours ahead and keep it refrigerated, but add the boiling water just before baking to preserve the cocoa bloom. The baked cake can be made a day in advance and stored covered at room temperature or refrigerated.

FAQ

Can I use butter instead of vegetable oil in this recipe?
Butter can be substituted, but it will change the texture and moisture level. Oil keeps the cake moist because it stays liquid at room temperature, while butter solidifies and can create a firmer crumb. If you use butter, melt it first and expect a slightly denser cake.
Why is boiling water added to the batter?
Boiling water blooms the cocoa powder, releasing more intense chocolate flavor and helps dissolve it fully. It also thins the batter, which leads to a moist, tender cake. Skipping this step results in a less flavorful, denser cake.
How do I know when the cake is done without overbaking?
Start checking at 30 minutes by inserting a toothpick in the center. It should come out mostly clean with a few moist crumbs. The cake should spring back lightly to the touch. Overbaking dries it out, so err on the side of slightly underdone.
Can I make this cake gluten-free?
Yes! Substitute the flour with a gluten-free baking blend that contains xanthan gum. Baking times may vary slightly, and texture might be a bit denser but still delicious. Be sure to test doneness carefully.
What’s the best way to store leftover cake?
Store leftovers wrapped tightly with plastic wrap or in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or in the fridge for up to 5 days. To keep it moist, avoid leaving it uncovered. You can also freeze it for longer storage.
Can I use non-dairy sour cream or yogurt?
Non-dairy sour cream or yogurt can work but may affect texture and flavor slightly. Choose a thick, full-fat plant-based sour cream for best results. The cake might be a bit less tender or flavorful depending on the brand.
Why is my cake dense instead of fluffy?
Common causes include overmixing the batter, not using room temperature ingredients, or inaccurate measuring of baking soda or flour. Mix gently, measure carefully, and ensure your ingredients are at room temperature for best rise and texture.
Can I double this recipe?
Yes, doubling works well. Use two pans or a larger pan and adjust baking time accordingly. Start checking doneness at the original time, but expect it may take longer if the batter is deeper.

What You'll Need

One 9-inch round cake, 8 servings

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder
  • 1/2 cup sour cream, room temperature
  • 1 large egg, room temperature
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/2 cup boiling water
  • 1/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • Pinch of salt

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease a 9-inch round cake pan. Greasing the pan well and lightly dusting it with flour helps the cake release easily after baking. This step is crucial to avoid any sticking, which can ruin the cake’s appearance and texture. You can also line the bottom with parchment paper for extra insurance. Make sure the oven is fully preheated before putting the cake in, so it bakes evenly from the start.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Oven is preheated to 350°F and pan is well greased with no dry spots
Step 2
In a large bowl, whisk together the flour, sugar, cocoa powder, baking soda, and salt. Sifting the flour and cocoa helps remove lumps and aerates the mixture, which leads to a lighter cake. Mixing the dry ingredients first ensures they’re evenly distributed, so the cake bakes uniformly. Take your time here to avoid any clumps of cocoa powder, which can create bitter spots in the cake.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are fully combined and look smooth with no lumps
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk the sour cream, egg, vegetable oil, and vanilla extract until smooth. The sour cream adds moisture and a slight tang that balances the sweetness, while the oil keeps the cake tender. Whisking the wet ingredients thoroughly ensures they’ll blend easily with the dry mix, preventing streaks or uneven texture in the batter.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wet ingredients are fully combined into a smooth, homogeneous liquid
Step 4
Pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients and stir gently until just combined. Overmixing can develop gluten in the flour, making the cake dense and chewy instead of soft and tender. Use a spatula to fold the mixture carefully, stopping when you no longer see flour streaks. It’s okay if the batter looks a bit lumpy; that’s better than overworking it.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is mixed with no visible dry flour but still slightly lumpy
Step 5
Carefully pour the boiling water over the batter and stir gently until fully combined. This step might seem odd, but the hot water blooms the cocoa powder, intensifying the chocolate flavor and giving the cake a moist, tender crumb. Be cautious not to overmix here, just combine until uniform. The batter will be thin—that’s normal and helps create a moist cake.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is smooth, thin, and evenly chocolate-colored with no lumps
Step 6
Pour the batter into the prepared pan and smooth the top with a spatula. Smoothing the surface evenly helps the cake bake uniformly without doming or cracking. Tap the pan gently on the counter once or twice to release any large air bubbles trapped inside the batter, which can cause holes or uneven texture in the finished cake.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter surface is level and even with no large air bubbles
Step 7
Bake in the preheated oven for 30-35 minutes. Start checking at 30 minutes by inserting a toothpick into the center; it should come out mostly clean or with a few moist crumbs. Don’t overbake — it’s better to pull it out slightly underdone because it continues to cook as it cools. Overbaking dries the cake and ruins its tender crumb.
⏱ Time: 30-35 minutes
✓ Doneness: Toothpick inserted comes out with a few moist crumbs, cake springs back lightly when touched
Step 8
Allow the cake to cool in the pan for 10 minutes, then carefully invert onto a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling in the pan too long can make the bottom soggy from trapped steam. Removing it early lets air circulate and keeps the texture perfect. Use a thin knife to loosen edges if it sticks.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake is warm but not hot and releases easily from the pan
Step 9
Serve as is or dust with powdered sugar, frost with your favorite icing, or top with fresh berries. This cake is versatile and tastes amazing with simple toppings or rich frosting. I often enjoy it plain with a cup of coffee for a cozy afternoon treat. Store leftovers covered at room temperature or in the fridge.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake is fully cooled and ready to slice cleanly without crumbling
